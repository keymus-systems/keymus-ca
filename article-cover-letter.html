COVER LETTERS

How to Write a Cover Letter for Canadian Employers (With Templates)

Stand out with a compelling, professionally written cover letter

Published March 2026 8 min read Keymus Career Team

Many Canadian employers still request cover letters, particularly in government, education, non-profit, and professional sectors. While your resume lists your qualifications, a cover letter tells your story—why you're interested in this role, what specific value you bring, and why you're a great fit for this company. This guide teaches you how to write compelling Canadian cover letters that get you interviews.

Key Insight: In tech and startup sectors, cover letters are becoming optional. However, in government, education, and large corporations, they're still required. When requested, a strong cover letter significantly increases your interview chances.

Do You Actually Need a Cover Letter?

Cover Letters ARE Still Required If:

  • The job posting explicitly requests one
  • You're applying to government positions (almost always required)
  • You're entering education, non-profit, or healthcare sectors
  • The role involves writing or communication
  • You're applying to established corporations

Cover Letters Are OPTIONAL If:

  • The posting says "resume only" or doesn't mention cover letter
  • You're applying to tech companies or startups
  • The role is entry-level or trade-focused
  • You're applying through job boards without cover letter fields

Our recommendation: When in doubt, include a cover letter. It takes minimal extra time and can only help.

The Canadian Cover Letter Format

Structure (Top to Bottom)

  1. Your contact information (header)
  2. Date
  3. Employer's contact information
  4. Salutation
  5. Opening paragraph (hook)
  6. Body paragraph(s) (your qualifications)
  7. Closing paragraph (call to action)
  8. Sign-off

Length & Format

  • Length: 3–4 paragraphs, one page maximum
  • Font: Same as your resume (Arial, Calibri, Times New Roman, 10–12pt)
  • Spacing: Single-spaced with spaces between paragraphs
  • Margins: 0.5–1 inch on all sides
  • File format: Save as PDF to preserve formatting
  • File name: "FirstName_LastName_CoverLetter.pdf"

Writing Each Section: Detailed Guidance

Opening Paragraph: Hook Them (2–3 Sentences)

Your opening must be strong and specific. Avoid generic openers like "I am writing to apply for the position..."

Generic:
"I am interested in the accountant position at your company because I enjoy working with numbers."
Strong:
"Your recent expansion into Western Canada presents an exciting opportunity, and I'm eager to support it as your Senior Accountant. With 8 years optimizing financial reporting systems and a proven track record reducing audit timelines by 25%, I'm confident I can drive efficiencies across your growing team."

Body Paragraphs: Show Your Fit (4–6 Sentences Each)

Paragraph 1: Connect your experience directly to job requirements

Good Approach: Pick 1–2 requirements from the job posting and provide specific examples of how you've met or exceeded them. Include metrics whenever possible.

Paragraph 2: Demonstrate company research

Research Tips: Check the company's website, recent news, LinkedIn company page, and reviews. Reference specific initiatives, values, or challenges. This shows genuine interest—not a templated letter sent to 100 companies.

Closing Paragraph: Call to Action (2–3 Sentences)

End strong by reaffirming your interest and making it easy to contact you. Include phone and email.

"I would welcome the opportunity to discuss how my project management experience and proven ability to deliver on tight timelines can benefit [Company Name]. I am available for an interview at your convenience and can be reached at 416-555-1234 or jane.smith@email.com. Thank you for your consideration, and I look forward to speaking with you soon."

Key Tips for Canadian Cover Letters

1. Research the Hiring Manager's Name

  • LinkedIn search: Find the company and look for the hiring manager
  • Company website: Check the team page
  • Call the company: Ask "Who would be overseeing this role?" (gets you the direct name)
  • If you can't find the name: Use "Dear Hiring Team" (better than generic "To Whom It May Concern")

2. Mirror the Job Posting Language

If the posting says "We need someone with strong project management skills," use that exact phrase in your cover letter. This helps with both human readers and ATS screening.

3. Quantify Your Achievements

Numbers stand out. Instead of "improved sales," say "increased sales by 35% in 18 months."

4. Show Company Knowledge

Reference something specific about the company: a recent project, their mission, their values, or a market achievement. This demonstrates genuine interest.

5. Keep Tone Professional but Personable

Canadian culture values some warmth and authenticity. You can be professional without sounding robotic. Use "I" statements and write conversationally.

6. Proofread Ruthlessly

Spelling and grammar errors are instant rejection triggers. Proofread 3 times minimum. Ask someone else to read it before sending.

Do's and Don'ts for Canadian Cover Letters

DO:
  • Keep to one page
  • Be specific with examples
  • Research the company
  • Use the hiring manager's name
  • Include metrics/results
  • Tailor each letter
  • Use professional tone
DON'T:
  • Use generic templates without customizing
  • Repeat your resume word-for-word
  • Include personal information (age, marital status)
  • Mention salary expectations
  • Write negatively about previous employers
  • Exceed one page
  • Use informal language or slang

Industry-Specific Cover Letter Tips

Government/Public Sector

  • Be formal and structured
  • Reference specific job duties and requirements
  • Demonstrate understanding of bureaucratic processes
  • Highlight integrity and reliability

Nonprofit Sector

  • Show genuine passion for their mission
  • Demonstrate community involvement
  • Emphasize collaboration and teamwork
  • Highlight budget-conscious mindset

Tech/Startups

  • Keep tone more casual (if appropriate)
  • Show innovation and problem-solving mindset
  • Reference specific products or initiatives
  • Highlight adaptability and learning agility

Corporate/Large Enterprises

  • Maintain formal, professional tone
  • Reference company achievements and strategy
  • Demonstrate understanding of corporate culture
  • Show career growth ambition

Common Cover Letter Mistakes

Mistake #1: One-Size-Fits-All Template

Sending the same letter to every employer signals lack of interest. Customize each letter with the company name, role, and specific examples.

Mistake #2: Repeating Your Resume

Don't summarize your resume. Instead, tell the story of why you're passionate about this specific role and how you'll add value.

Mistake #3: Too Much Personal Information

Avoid mentioning age, marital status, religion, or personal hobbies. Focus on professional qualifications.

Final Checklist Before Submitting

Final Review:
  • ☐ Personalized with hiring manager's name
  • ☐ Company name and specific role mentioned
  • ☐ One page maximum
  • ☐ No spelling or grammar errors
  • ☐ Keywords from job posting included
  • ☐ Specific examples with metrics
  • ☐ Professional tone maintained
  • ☐ Saved as PDF with professional name
  • ☐ Contact information included
  • ☐ Proofread by someone else
